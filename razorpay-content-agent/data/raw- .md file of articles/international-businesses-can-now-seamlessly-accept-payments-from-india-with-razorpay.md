India is one of the largest and fastest growing economies in the world. With its enormous domestic market, India is increasingly becoming a preferred destination for global businesses, who want to accept payments from India.

The Union government has spoken of India’s digital economy growing from about $200 billion in 2017 to between $800 billion and $1 trillion by 2030. This would imply that the share of the digital economy [would](https://theprint.in/opinion/digital-india-a-good-start-but-govt-must-build-capabilities-for-robust-growth/780327/)[grow](https://www.livemint.com/economy/indias-digital-economy-to-see-exponential-growth-to-800-bn-by-2030-fm-11647014673827.html) from about 8 per cent to 18-23 percent inside the next decade.

To enable this, the government has taken numerous measures to facilitate an easy market entry and promote global collaborations. In addition to these reforms, the increase in international business opportunities in India has stemmed from technological disruption, internet penetration, and rising incomes.

However, accepting payments from Indian customers has been a pain point for international businesses. The existing channels of accepting payments have significant limitations and require a great amount of time and effort for activation.
